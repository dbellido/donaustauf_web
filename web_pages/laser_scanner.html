Lasser scanner

Laser scanners are instruments able to produce distance measurements at every point in every direction by the controlled steering of laser beams (Marshall, 2004). They can acquire data fromlarge tosmall scales dependingontheir operation principleandthey are used in different applications such as surveying, navigation, virtual/augmented reality or deformation monitoring (Alkan and Karsidag, 2012; Sternberg and Kersten, 2007). The field of laser scanner technology is a rapidly developing and instruments, software and platforms are being improved constantly (Fr√∂hlich and Mettenleiter, 2004). From a cloud point, which is the main output of this technology, different products can be delivered as well as be used in combination with other sensors (Wang, 2011). This first section aims to summarize and introduce the evolution of the technology, thereafter the current state of the laser scanner technology will be explained. On the one hand the main advantage of using a laser scanner is the rapid acquisition of the object as well as the possibility to acquire remote and complex areas, reducing the time and the associated costs in comparison with other techniques, such as traditional surveying approaches (Wang, 2011; Hyypp√§, 2011). In addition to this, the acquisition is largely independent of illumination (Alkan and Karsidag, 2012). Furthermore, the new features included in the latest generations of laser scanner have reduced the acquisition time and simplified the registration and processing of the point cloud.

On the other hand, a laser scanner is far from being perfect. Errors in the measurement are produced by several factors, such as the environmental conditions and the permeability or the roughness of the target surface (Alkan and Karsidag, 2012). Determination of the accuracy of these instruments is not an easy task due to the huge amount of measurement performed in each scan. On this basis different researches have studied the accuracy and reliability of laser scanner instruments, such as (Kersten and Mechelke, 2009; Y. Zhang,Wu, Cheng, and Liu, 2008; BoehlerandMarbs, 2003; Tsakiri, Pagounis,andArabatzi, 2015).

Currently, any acquisition technique is not isolated and several techniques are used in a wide range of applications. Additionally, large data sets are produced. Neverthless, the management of such size of data is a challenge due to tasks such as noise filtering, automatic registration, mesh reduction or visulazation. On this basis the improvement of the processing is another important topic, eitherby the integration with other sensors (Pandey,McBride, Savarese, and Eustice, 2010; Moussa, 2014)or the automatic registration of different data sets (Previtali, Barazzetti, Brumana and Scaioni, 2014).

Laser scanners are widely used in CulturalHeritage because this technology is able to achieve most of the requirements in the data acquisition (Figure 1). Furthermore, the high variety of the scanner instruments allows the usage in acquiring geometric 3D data in different environments, such as small artefacts (English Heritage, 2011; Reznicek and Pavelka, 2008), complex buildings (Yamada, Takase, Shimoda, and Nakagawa, 2002; Cheng, Yang, and Yen, 2015) or large landscapes (Evans et al., 2013; Verhoeven, 2011).

The laser scanner techniques used for surveying have different names, such as LS (Laser Scanning), LiDAR(Light Detection andRanging), or ALS (Aerial Laser Scanning) and TLS (Terrestrial Laser Scanning) depending on it is based on aerial or terrestrial mobile platforms. The invention of the first laser took place in 1958 and since then laser technologies began to be used in surveying applications within the military industry. In 1994 the first commercial aerial laser scanner was available (Petzold, Reiss, and St√∂ssel, 1999). Later on, the TLS, came on the market (Hyypp√§, 2011). According to (Staiger, n.d.), the TLS development has been classified in different stages. The first TLS were introduced to the market in 1997, with models such as the first Cyrax scanner and the first RIEGL Scanner LMS Z210. Theyworked with a pulse based principle and the data storage and power supplywere external. In 2002, the first phase based scanner broke into the market. Also the scanners became faster and easier to transport. After 2007, the data storage, the power supply aswell as the scanner measurement components are mounted in the same structure. Additionally, other sensors such as GNSS are also incorporated. Therefore, it allowed the direct integration with surveying methods. Furthermore, digital images started to be used in order to add color to the point clouds. The 4th generation, starting in 2009, uses compact scanners and the digital camera is fully integrated in the acquisition and processing. The classification of the point cloud is possible as well, due to the FullWave Form Analysis is introduced (Ullrich and Pfennigbauer, 2011).

Currently, the improvement of different platforms and the new capacities of IMU-GNSS systems allow the integration of different sensor, including a laser scanner mounted on board vehicle. It is called MLS (Mobile Terrestrial Laser Scanning) (Hyypp√§, 2011; Olteanu, Badea, & Moldoveanu, 2010). Consequently, different possibilities have appeared using different platforms; hand scanners such as T SCAN CS or bagpack solutions such as Leica Pegasus BagPack. As a conclusion, Laser scanning technology is a wide field. The different scanning devices can either be separated by their working principles or their sensors used. Each scanner is specifically designed for a different application, a different scale and different precision or different environmental conditions.



ITN-DCH project aims to analyze, design, research, develop and validate the innovative muti-disciplinary and intersectorial research training framework that covers the whole lifecycle of digital CH research for a cost-effective preservation, documentation, protection and presentation. Donaustauf castle is one of the case studies where the different fellow are applying their research. With the support of ArcTron3D, the Marie Curie fellows are being trained in laser scanner and light structured methods in Cultural Heritage for high resolutions 3D surveys.

Photogrammetry

Photogrammetry is the science of creating 3D information from photographs. Nowadays, just a commercial camera is required to acquire the images. From terrestrial or aerial plattformas photogrammetry is one of the most suitable techniques in the digiization of Cultural Heritage.

Laser scanner

The purpose of a 3D laser scanner is to create a point cloud of the surface of a objetc. Nowadays there a wide amount of scanners in the market and they can acquire information in small and large scenes. The three principles of laser scanners include Time of flight (TOF), Phase Shift and Triangulation-based systems.

Structured light scanner

A structured light scanner is a 3D intruments to achieve the 3D shape of a object. It uses projection light patterns and a camera system (usually two cameras). Projection band of lights onto an object, produces line distorted from the different perspectives, and it is used to extract the 3D information about the object.
