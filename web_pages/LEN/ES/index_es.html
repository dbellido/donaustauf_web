Proyecto Donaustauf

Proyecto Donaustauf se enmarca dentro del proyecto conocido como “Redes Iniciales de Formación para la Digitalización de Patrimonio Cultural: Poryectando el pasdo en el futuro", normalmente bajo el acrónimo en inglés de ITN-DCH project. El proyecto a la red europea de formación conocido como "Marie-Curie" bajo la temática de la documentación, conservación y protección de patrimonio cultural, y que a su vez pertenece al marco europeo de investigación "FP7 PEOPLE". El proyecto comenzó el primero de Octubre de 2013 y está formado por un consorcio de 14 instituciones principales y 10 instutuciones asociados cubriendo un gran abánico de organizaciones, desde universidades, a centros de investigación, empresas, librerías, museos o archivos. El proyecto busca la formación de 20 investigadores, 16 jóvenes (en étapa predoctoral) y 4 sénior (en etapa postdoctoral) con el objetivo de crear una red sólida de investigadores en la conservaicón de patrimonio y al mismo tiempo, dotarlos de las competencias necesarias para consolidar sus capacidades en el mercado laboral una vez acabado el proyecto.

ITN-DCH project tiene como objetivo el análisis, diseño, investigación, desarrollo y validación dentro de un marco innovativo, multidisciplinar e intersectorial que cubre el ciclo completo de digitalización de patrimonio histórico y cultural. El principal motivo para ello, es que este patrimonio es un elemento vertebrador de la identidad europea y su conexión con otras culturas, siendo además un importante valor para el crecimiento económico y la creación de empleo. Consecuentemente, la conservación, documentación, digitalización y musealización son elementos esenciales en los que están implicadas diversas disciplinas. Para ello ITN-DCH busca encarar la innovación e investigación en estas tareas desde una perspectiva multidisciplinar, combinando disciplinas diferentes en un único proyecto y con un carácter intersectorial aunando sectores como el universitario, el investigador o el empresarial.

ITN-DCH engloba las diferentes variables de patrimonio, tanto tangible (libros, periódicos, imágenes, cuadros, manuscritos, vestuario, yacimientos arqueológicos, objetos históricos o monumentos), como intangible (música, danzas tradicionales, teatro o información verbal) así como la relación entre ambos. Al mismo tiempo, ITN-DCH tiene como objetivo no sólo la documentación sino la puesta en valor del patrimonio. Para ello, el proyecto acentúa la importancia de la divulgación en diferentes campos (protección de patrimonio, educación, turismo, industria, moda, cine, música, publicidad, videojuegos o televisión) a partir de la investigación en la personalización de nuevas técnicas de diseminación y realidad aumentada (i), en nuevas técnicas de adquisición de datos tridimensionales (ii), nuevas formas de representación 3D y 4D (iii) así como la interoperabilidad y nuevos estándares para el archivo y la transferencia de información (iv).

Para hacer visible las diferentes actividades e investigación realizada por los diferentes miembros del proyecto se han seleccionado 4 casos de estudio:

ITN-DCH CASO DE ESTUDIO I: Iglesia de Panagia Asinou Church (Nicosia, Cyprus)
ITN-DCH CASO DE ESTUDIO II: Carnuntum (Carnuntum, Austria)
ITN-DCH CASO DE ESTUDIO III: Castillo de Donaustauf (Alemania)
ITN-DCH CASO DE ESTUDIO IV: Ilmendorf (Archäologische Staatssammlung München, Alemania)

Este web tiene como objetivo presenter las diferentes actividades que se han llevado a cabo en la documentación del castillo de Donaustauf (III). Del mismo modo, las diferentes opciones dentro de la página servirán para la divulgación de la historia del castillo y su estado actual, sirviéndose de diferentes estudios realizados por los investigadores. Del mismo modo, visores de imágenes panorámicas (2D) y de nubes de puntos o mallas (3D), dotarán al visitante de la posibilidad de visitar el castillo de manera virtual a través de las nuevas herramientas disponibles en la web.



Historia

East of Regensburg (southern Germany), the township of Donaustauf is situated. The small city is dominated by the ruins of the castle Donaustauf. The castle sits on a hilltop, approximately 100m above the Danube. It is the oldest castle mentioned in documents within the area of Regensburg. Between 914 and 930, several records can be found that mention a castle which is called Stauff (castellum quod dicitur Stuffo). It is assumed that the castle was erected by Bishop Tuto of Regensburg (893–930), as a defence against the Hungarian invasion. However, archaeological excavations revealed that at least the North-Eastern part of the hilltop was fortified about 500 B.C. already.

Since the 10th century, the castle has had an eventful history: Just between 1132 and 1161 it was seized four times. Finally, the Donaustauf castle was sieged and conquered by Swedish troops in 1634. They destroyed the castle over several days of artillery fire, and explosions. After that, repairs were done but since 1648 it has been abandoned and used as a quarry. A cemetery is located within the outer castle walls, which is still in use today.

Over the centuries, sector fortifications have been built to protect the castle. In other words, the hill on which the castle stands was fortified from top to bottom and this fortification was adapted to the different needs of the time. The oldest part dates back to the 11th century, the last strengthening was carried out in 1620.

To protect the central part of the castle, six gates were built (A-F). Only four of them are visible today. The most impressive gate is the most inner gate to the bailey castle (gate F). (Figure 2). The entrance is covered by two groin vaults that are separated by a wide arch. In the first floor of the building even today the castle chapel is still visible.



Planificación de la toma de datos

Based on the field experience by ArcTron3D, a code of practise for digitisation is presented. The airborne technology will be used to create a model of the hilltop and the landscape. The landscape model will include the hilltop of the castle, the Danube, the town and part of the backcountry. The data-acquisition will be done using the ultra light paraglide trike and a Nikon D800E camera. To create a valid and useable result one will define a flight path before to ensure good coverage and overlapping of the pictures. There is already an existing 3D model. This dataset, which is available within ArcTron3D, can be used as a reference dataset by the fellows. Depending on the resolution and the size of the area the cameras and lenses to be used as well as the flight altitude and duration should be taken into consideration among other important factors.

A ground plan using GPS sensors, laser scanner and total stations could also be useful to verify and update existing plans as well as to georeference the object for further detailed studies of the chapel and the gate.

Made of Romanesque fabric, Gate F is one of the most interesting parts of the castle. Therefore, a detailed and precise documentation of it should be done. Different methods of terrestrial laser scanning, terrestrial photogrammetry and even airborne photogrammetry can be applied.

On the preserved walls of the chapel there are traces of painting, dating back to the 12th century. Since the walls of the chapel have recently been whitewashed it is not longer possible to see these tracings. However, there are drawings in books available to reconstruct the scenes on the wall. Given the available equipment in the consortium it will be interesting to see if one is able to discover the paintings underneath the whitewash. The paintings itself, as far as they have been documented yet, depicts for persons exchanging a book-shape like thing. Above there is a Latin inscription as well, referring to the Virgin Walpurga (known as Saint Walpurga 710-777/779). It might be interesting to try to figure out the whole story of the painting and the story that is depicted. Linking the paintings of the chapel wall with other content or even bringing the figures back to life will be a challenge for the task that concern the intangible CH part of the project.

The chapel itself is of great historical value, being one of the few Romanesque chapels within the region since it still has some of the original plaster such as capitals, columns, arches, etc. Structured light scanning (SLS) methods in comparison to photogrammetry and infrared/hyperspectral imaging can be applied to document this part.

4D models of the gate would also be a possible task for the project fellows. 2D-Content such as the Merian sketch , can be brought into the project as well. Also there are some rough sketches by the American painter Joseph Mallord William Turner (1775-1851) and other artists . So it should not be a problem to try to rebuild the gate over time based on research that has been done, the available sketches and the researched realised by the fellows. This will bring in the 4th dimension: “time”.
