Donaustauf is a small market town located in the southeast of Germany. Specifically, Donaustauf belongs to the region of Upper Palatinate (Oberpfalz in German), in the state of Bavaria (Bayern). It is part of the Regensburg district ("Landkreis" in German) which main city is around 5 km to the west.

The territory is bathed in the south by the waters of the Danube River and at the same time is located at the foothills of the Bavarian forest. Two monuments preside over the city and dominate the landscape perched in two hills. The Donaustauf castle, which is the subject of this web, is visible in the middle of the village while the Walhalla is in the east of the urban centre. The Walhalla a Teutonic temple, built up as a reproduction of the Parthenon. This impressive monument as well as the privileged views offered from it attract the attention of thousands of tourists, especially during the summertime period.

History

The history of the village would hardly be conceivable without understanding the significant role that Donaustauf castle played in the growth of the region until its destruction in the 17th century. The other important point that should be taken into account is the Danube River, which baths Donaustauf. The river, Donau in German, names the town. Geographically, it separates two different regions, the south region, which was inhabited and developed at the time of the conquest of the Bavarians, while the northern area was only made habitable by deforestation. Furthermore, it was the north border of the Roman Empire and it has had important commercial routes since ancient time. An import Roman military camp (Castra Regina) was settled up in Regensburg and the salt trade was an important activity in the area through the centuries.

Concerning the area of Donaustauf, the oldest findings of a population can be found in Sulzbach, it can be considered as a different area through the history due to its incorporation to Donaustauf administrative center has occurred in recent times. Furthermore, the oldest administrative centre was probably located in Sulzbach.

Focused on the surrounding area of the current urban center, the earliest findings that denote a permanent population dated from the year 500 before Christ on and they have been located in the hill where the ruins of the castle are still until today. Therefore, it was proved during the excavations performed in 1981/82, when the foundations of a fortress (presumably inhabited by the Celts) were brought to light.

The construction of the castle took place probably between 914 and 930. This initial castle aimed to protect the nearby town of Regensburg from the Hungarian invasions and was commissioned by Bishop Tuto. Tuto was the ninth bishop of Regensburg as well as head of St. Emmeram's Abbey. The territory of Donaustauf was given as a donation of the King Conrad I to St. Emmeram Abbey . After the separation of the Abbey and the bishopric, the territory went in 1031 to the diocese.

Until the 13th century Donaustauf could be kept in the possession of the Regensburger bishops, although straggles between diocese, King and Dukes were frequents. The first known rule about Donaustauf is dated in 1285. Rudolf von Habsburg confirmed county rights for Donaustauf. In the 14th and 15th century the change of owner's continued, Emperor Charles IV, Deke Otto V and Duke Stephen were some of the owner's among others. In 1486, Donaustauf and together with Regensburg, went into the hands of the Duke of Bavaria, Albrecht IV. The market rights were bestowed to Donautauf ten years later. In 1592 a lightning struck in the castle tower and the entire system has been greatly affected.

The Swedish invasion is one of the phases of the "30 Years' War". This phase ravaged central-Europe from 1630 to 1635. Swedish-protestant army invaded Bavaria, crushed Bavarian troops and occupied the region forcing to pay taxes. In opposition, different revolts erupted in opposition to the taxation. In 1633, the Swedish troops invaded Regensburg. In this context, the Bavarian troops stationed in the castle intercepted a convoy of 60 truckloads salt supplies for the occupied area. In retaliation, Swedish troops led by Bernard of Saxe-Weimar, captured the castle and destroyed it mostly. Although the plant of the castle was poorly repaired, the castle has been in ruins since then.

In 1710 Donaustauf returned to the Bishopric of Regensburg. In 1803 was incorporated into the new created Archbishopric of Regensburg, also known such as Principality of Regensburg. In 1810 came to Bavaria, according to the Treaty of Paris. The complete Donaustauf is acquired by the Princes of Thurn and Taxis in 1812, who owns the title of Duke of Wörth an der Donau since 1899th. An important palace was built for the Thurn and Taxis family in 1843, but it fell in 1880 to a huge fire together with the 60% of the buildings in the village. Louis I of Bavaria (Ludwig I) who was an admirer of ancient Greece, patronized arts and different neoclassical building. Among them, the Walhalla was built up in 1842 is still today an important tourist attraction.

Currently Donaustauf has about 4000 inhabitants, including the municipalities of Altenthann, Bach a.d. Donau and Sulzbach, which was incoporated in 1978. Different important buildings as well as Walhalla are; a sanatorium built up in 1908 still in use known as "Klinik Donaustauf", St salvator church dated in 1388, the late baroque temple consecrated in 1730 to St Michael and a Chinese Tower which was built in 1842 in order to coincide with the Walhalla opening.



Monuments

Parochial church of St. Michael

The St Michael parish church is located on the southern slope of the castle hill, specifically in Albertus-Magnus-Weg 5, and as all the town its history is linked to the history of the castle. Since 1245 Donaustauf was an independent parish. Therefore a medieval parish church was located in the space where the actual St Michael church is today. In the 14th century Donaustauf was chosen as one of the four deanships and thus one of the most important parishes of the bishopric. The initial construction was destroyed after the sieges of the castle in 1388 and rebuilt again 1397.

The records of the 16th century brought to light clearer recordings of the parish and even to the political history of Donaustauf. One important figure in the deanships was Otto Heinrich Bachmair, who was additionally auxiliary bishop of Regensburg from 1622 to 1634.

Together with the castle, the church was ravaged by war and disaster and the rectory burned down. During the Thirty Years War the parish was burned. According to the different recordings, the church had different predecessors. Before the current building, the church was dedicated to St. Florian and it was ruined by a fire in 1724.

Commissioned by the Pastor Georg Friedrich Weinzierl, the current late baroque complex was built up. It was consecrated in 1703 to the patron St. Michael. The octagonal tower visible today corresponds in his foundation of an older arrangement. The cemetery walls located next to the current structure are probably from the 18th century. In 1860 the church had already five altars and four bells. A pulpit and three new altars were commissioned by Pastor Wallner (1913-1931). These were designed by Schmalz architect from Munich and implemented by Loibl sculptor from Stadtamhof (Regensburg).

Additionally, the church has four bells available which from a 4 voice Salve Regina. The three smaller date back to 1945 and they were made by Charles Hamm in Regensburg, while the great bell was poured in Regensburg by Georg Hofweber.

Pilgrimage Church of St. Salvator

The church is located in Sulzbach an der Donau (currently integrated in Donaustauf). It holds an important value due it conserves interesting, valuable objects from different eras as well as because of its pilgrimage history. The pilgrimage is based in a robbery took place in 1388 during one of the sieges of the Donaustauf castle. Three soldiers robbed the communion of the church of Sulzbach and they hide them in the forest. Once the communion wafers were found, many pilgrims started to pray in the location where the communion wafers were hidden and it made the building of a church necessary.

St. Salvator church is formed by a hall building with a choir, eastern vestry and west tower with tent roof and portico. The initial structure was built around 1430 in the Gothic style. In 1607 the choir and long house were added. The stair arrangement is post medieval. In the 18th century it was renovated in order to adopt a baroque style. With the construction of the Walhalla, the Bavarian king Ludwigs I order the transformation of the church in the same neoclassical architectural style. The pilgrimage occurred mainly in the 14th and 15th century. According to different references the phenomenon experienced a big growth, and even the church was not enough to accommodate all the visitors. Therefore a tent was placed in front of the church in order to hold part of the service.

The complete event is described extensively by Aventin (1534) and the Munich court librarian Felix Andreas Öfele (1780). Furthermore, eight valuable wood board paintings (1,4×2,00m) from an unknown artist described very clearly the events with additional captions. They are nowadays located in St. Salvator and they are especially interesting for the study of the clothes.

The church suffered (as in the rest of the region) ravage during the Thirty Years War. In 1633 the church minister reported that the church was broken open repeatedly by the Swedish troops. In 1635 reparations were made in windows and doors. The damages caused by the Swedish troops were also reported in 1642 when they stole 253 florins from it. Nevertheless, the pilgrimage didn´t stop as it is expressed in the different contributions made to other churches. From the 17th century is the statue of Saint Salvator, which after the last renovation, stands in the middle of the church, just in the place where the communion wafers were hidden. The bells of Saint Salvator also come from this time. They were poured 1643 in Straubing and they are still today hand operated.

Structural changes were experienced by the building in the 18th century in order to be adapted to the baroque style. At that time 13 ceiling paintings were created by Otto Gebhard (pupil of the brothers Asam) following the theme of the Eucharist. Additionally, a baroque or rococo elegant altar has been added. It was sculpted by Anton Neu from Prüfening. The altarpiece shows Christ with his disciples in Emmaus while the legend by a pelican. The rococo organ as well as the pulpit dated also from the first part of the 18th century.

The flow of pilgrims was decreased. At the beginning of the 19th century the Walhalla was built. Under the idea of a harmonious view in the Danube formed by the Donaustauf castle the St. Salvator church and the Walhalla, the exterior appearance was strongly transformed. The goal was to lead back the church to its original Bizantine aspect by removing the different baroque elements. Tower, roof and windows were transformed to accomplish this goal. The restoration was according the new neo-Romanesque style, common in Germany in the beginning of the 19th century which was inspired in the 11th-12th Romanesque architecture. Leo von Klenze, same architect than in the Walhalla, was the responsible of the renovation.

In the restoration done in 1970-1980, original paintings were discovered under a 5 cm thick cleaning layer which could be got in bigger range in the west part of the church. These are outstanding works of an Italian artist, painted in 1400, a rare example of its kind of art in Bavaria. The paintings show scenes from the New Testament. The well-preserved fresco right of the main entrance of the church shows the flagellation of Christ.

Walhalla

The Walhalla is a neoclassical temple, building in the same style as Parthenon and built up in the 19th century. It aims to be ne used as a hall of fame that honors distinguished and relevant names in German history

To understand the context when it was built, the view should come back to the beginning of the 19th century. In that time, specifically in 1806, Napoleon had annexed German lands along the Rhine River and the North Sea to the First French Empire. Furthermore, central German forces formed the Rhine’s confederation in the side of Napoleon. At the same time Francis II, dissolves the Holy Roman Empire after the defeat against Napoleon forces in the battle of Austerlitz and he instead crowned himself as Emperor of Austria. Francis II continued his leading role as an opponent of Napoleonic France in the Napoleonic Wars, and suffered several more defeats after Austerlitz.

During 1806-1807 German forces were split in opposition or in favour of Napoleon during the War of the Fourth Coalition, which terminated with the triumph of Napoleon. During this time, Bavaria behaved as a satellite state of Napoleon and in gratefulness he elevated from Electorate to Kingdom and consequently Maximilian I was crowned as the first Bavarian king. In 1813, after the battle of Leipzig, the Bavarian Kingdom left the Confederation of the Rhine and joined the Sixth Coalition in the war against Napoleon keeping its sovereignty as kingdom. Finally, in 1815 the German Confederation was formed by the association of 39 states in central Europe. The heir to the throne, Crown Prince Ludwig was an enthusiastic about the common German history. As proof of this, his passionate declaration of war against Napoleon written together with Marshal von Wrede in 1814.

In 1807, Prince Ludwig started with the idea of bringing together the common German heritage and history. In order to pursue this idea, he commissioned different sculptors to create busts of relevant names in German history. The first of them, was a bust of Copernicus created by Johann Gottfried Schadow. Having into account the names proposed by the Swiss historian Johannes von Müller, 60 busts were completed when the Prince was crowned as King Ludwig I of Bavaria in 1825. In lodge to establish and preserve the busts, the King decides to establish a memorial commissioned and he commissioned this task to Leo von Klenze, one of the most important neoclassical architects in the 19th century.

The first stone of the monument was placed in 1830 in a position high above the Danube in the east of Donaustauf and 10 km away of the city of Regensburg. The monument was finished in 1842 and named as Walhalla that means paradise in Norse mythology. Inspired by the Parthenon, Leo von Klenze designed a building in the form of a temple surrounded by a portico. The combination of the colonnaded temple, the massive substructure required to support the complex and an original interior preserves the monument to be a copy of the Greek monument. Both pediment friezes show relevant events in German history, while the southern features the creation of the German Confederation in 1815, the northern is ornamented with scenes from the Battle of the Teutoburg Forestin 9 after ChristD.

At the inauguration of the monument, 96 busts and 64 plaques for those which a portrait was not available. The German Tongue was the selection criterion for the chosen of the bust, however a wider range is included. That’s why before 1871, with the formation of a German state, German refers to Germanic, including Gothic, Langobardic, Anglo-Saxon, Austrian, Dutch and Swiss. Therefore personalities from countries far away from the current Germany has a bust there. Whereas the Valhalla of Norse mythology served as home to those gloriously slain in battle, Ludwig intended his Walhalla not only for warriors, but also for scientists, writers, and clerics, and specifically included both men and women. National Socialist organizations, such as “Kraft durch Freude” promotes Walhalla as a cultural visit. In 1937, Hitler unveiled a bust of the Austrian compositor Bruckner and the number of visitors went to 131 520. Walhalla was also an important point in the Second World War, when the allies forces. In 1945 the 14th reaches the north bank of the Danube and began an assault the positions in Donaustauf and Walhalla commanded by the known general George Smith Patton, Jr. Currently the government of the Free State of Bavaria (Freistaat Bayern) is who decided the new additions, with the condition that the personality had died unless 20 years before. Only 31 busts have been added since its opening. Since 1962 new busts have been added to the original 96 at intervals of five to seven years. The selection is made by the Bavarian Council of Ministers, advised by the Bavarian Academy of Sciences. The last busts have been dedicated to Sophie Scholl (2003), a Munich student who was executed in 1943 for her non-violent resistance to the system, Edith Stein (2009), nun and philosophe died in a concentration camp, and Heinrich Heine who was a German romantic poet whose works were set to music by Strauss, Wagner and Brahms.

The breathtaking monument still catches the attention of the visitors because of its architecture as well as the amazing sights of the landscape. The visitor can get the Walhalla easily from Donaustauf , or from Regensburg by , , (Line 5) or , within a 2 hours trip.

Chinese Tower

The original wooden structure of the Chinese tower dates back to 1842, when Prince Maximilian Karl von Thurn und Taxis built up the tower in the summer residence of his family located in Donaustauf. Its construction was made to coincide with the inauguration of the Walhalla. However, it replaced a similar tower, which had been established by his father Maximilian Karl's father Karl Alexander von Thurn und Taxis. This old construction was located in the garden in the tie of china fashion as a reproduction of a structure of an imperial Chinese house.

The building of the tower follows the Chinese style in the second half of the 18th century, where replicas of the Chinese art and garden architecture are reproduced in small constructions in German, English and French parks. Another example of a wooden Chinese tower can be found in the English Garden in Munich, even so, it is a replica due to the original was destroyed during the Second World War. It gives a unique value to the Donaustauf tower due to it is the only preserved wooden 19th century “Chinese” structure in Germany.

The Chinese Tower is a Pagoda. These constructions are part of Buddhist temples in Chine. Despite of their meticulous aesthetics, they have a practical function being used to live, rest or cultural activities such as meditation, calligraphy as well as music and theatre events. The pagodas are ornamented with detail symbols with the goal to bring to the visitor good luck, health, wealth and longevity. This symbology is preserved in Donaustauf. A great fire which ravaged the town of Donaustauf and destroyed the summer palace in 1880. However, the fire didn’t affect the tower. In 1899 the family acquired the Prüfening palace in Regensburg and it was established as their new summer residence. Then the tower was dismantled and rebuilt again in 1902 in the gardens of the new summer residence. The tower was used for the family as a music and a tea pavilion until the Second World War.

In 1998 an association for the Chinese Tower was created. In 1999 the tower was dismantled, restored and moved to its location in Donaustauf according the Chinese tradition. Currently the Chinese tower is used for different events such as music, social celebrations or events of the association.

Thurn und Taxis

The Princely house of Thurn and Taxis is a German noble family who managed the main role in the postal services during the 16th century. There are also known because of their possessions, especially breweries, castles and palaces.

The aristocratic family is originally from Bergamo, Italy, under the name of Tasso. They moved to Germany and they were designed as Count (Grafen in German) in 1624. In 1650 they changed their name to “Della Torre e Tasso” which “Thurn und Taxis” is the German translation, used since now to name the family. The Holy Roman emperor Leopold I, gave them the title of Princes.

Ruggiano de Tasses founded a postal service in Italy in the last of the 15th century and in 1489 the monopoly of this service was awarded for the complete Holy Roman Empire. Despite the numerous conflicts and wars in central Europa the postal service was extended to new routes in Brussels, France, Spain or Rome by using a huge network of messengers and post-office around Europe.

1743 Prince Alexander Ferdinand was by Charles VII appointed Imperial principal commissioner and later on was confirmed by Francis I. Due to the Imperial Diet was located in Regensburg, the Thurn and Taxis moved their residence to this city.

Over the centuries the postal service was lost in pieces. The Spanish network was bought by the crown, while the German service was purchased by Prussia after the fall of the Free City f Frankfurt in 1866. Despite that they have preserved a large net of possessions in different countries, especially castles and palaces.

In 1810, the territory of Donaustauf, with the restriction of the sovereignty, was given to the Princes of Thurn and Taxis as an indemnity for the imperial posts, which had been retired by the Kingdom of Bavaria.. In 1815, the family received as well the imperial Abbey of St. Emmeram, since then their official residence. In 1899 are titled as Duke to Wörth an der Donau and Donaustauf. Furthermore, the Donaustauf castle belonged to the family until 1986. Therefore, the Thurn and Taxis family has become essential to understand the history of Donaustauf, especially in the last 200 hundred years.

In 1843, the summer residence palace was finished in Donaustauf. The castle was located close to the current position of the village, it was four stories high and contained a series of drawing-rooms. The place was completely destroyed together with almost the complete village in a big fire produced in 1880.

Close to the palace was located the hunting forest attached to the family. The similar to park and landscape with forester`s houses and hunting lodges encloses an area of 2.800 hectares in which, among other things lynxes, wild boars, red deer, beaver, otter, black storks, scrapper-cleanings, yellow belly toads and toads live. A golf club is nowadays located in the area.

ITN-DCH project aims to analyze, design, research, develop and validate the innovative muti-disciplinary and intersectorial research training framework that covers the whole lifecycle of digital CH research for a cost-effective preservation, documentation, protection and presentation. Photogrammetry

Photogrammetry is the science of creating 3D information from photographs. Nowadays, just a commercial camera is required to acquire the images. From terrestrial or aerial plattformas photogrammetry is one of the most suitable techniques in the digiization of Cultural Heritage.

Laser scanner

The purpose of a 3D laser scanner is to create a point cloud of the surface of a objetc. Nowadays there a wide amount of scanners in the market and they can acquire information in small and large scenes. The three principles of laser scanners include Time of flight (TOF), Phase Shift and Triangulation-based systems.

Structured light scanner

A structured light scanner is a 3D intruments to achieve the 3D shape of a object. It uses projection light patterns and a camera system (usually two cameras). Projection band of lights onto an object, produces line distorted from the different perspectives, and it is used to extract the 3D information about the object.
